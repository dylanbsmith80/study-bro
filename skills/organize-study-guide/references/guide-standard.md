# Study Guide Standard

Use this as the default format and completeness bar. Preserve a user-provided template when one exists.

## Required order

1. `# [Course/topic] — [exam/unit] Study Guide`
2. `## Scope and priorities`
3. One `##` section per unit or major theme
4. `## Cross-unit connections` when multiple units interact
5. `## Common confusions and distinctions`
6. `## Final review checklist`
7. `## Practice questions`
8. `## Answer key`
9. `## Source gaps and clarifications` only when needed

## Scope and priorities

State what material is covered and surface explicit instructor emphasis. Do not invent weighting. If priorities are inferred from repetition or detail, label them as inferred.

## Unit sections

Start each unit with a two- to four-sentence overview that gives the learner a mental model. Then use only the applicable patterns below.

### Concepts and key terms

Define each term on first use and explain its significance. A good entry answers: What is it? How does it work? Why does it matter? What is it commonly confused with?

### Processes and sequences

Use numbered steps. Include inputs, decision points, and outcomes when present. Preserve causal order rather than the order in which fragments appeared in the source.

### Comparisons

Use parallel bullets or a compact table with meaningful dimensions. Never create a table merely to hold long paragraphs.

### Formulas and quantitative rules

Show the formula, define every variable and unit, state when it applies, and include a source-grounded worked example if sufficient data exists. Do not invent numbers to force an example.

### People, cases, dates, or examples

Connect each item to the concept it demonstrates. Avoid isolated trivia unless the source signals it is testable.

## Completeness test

The guide is complete only when:

- Every distinct testable source concept appears or is explicitly listed as excluded or uncertain.
- Definitions are self-contained and do not rely on unexplained shorthand.
- Relationships, contrasts, and sequences are preserved, not flattened into disconnected bullets.
- Repeated source fragments are consolidated without discarding unique qualifiers or exceptions.
- Instructor-emphasized examples, warnings, and exceptions are retained.
- No outside facts are blended into source-grounded content without the user's approval and clear labeling.
- Practice questions sample every major unit, and every answer is recoverable from the guide.

## Practice question mix

Choose a count proportional to the source, normally 8–20 questions. Include at least:

- Two recall or definition questions
- Two explanation or cause-and-effect questions
- One comparison question when comparable concepts exist
- One application, scenario, sequence, or calculation question when applicable
- One synthesis question spanning multiple concepts when the source supports it

Number questions and mirror the numbering in the answer key. Keep answers concise but complete.

## Style

- Use direct, student-friendly language without becoming casual or vague.
- Preserve discipline-specific terminology while explaining it plainly.
- Keep bullets grammatically parallel.
- Use examples to clarify, not to decorate.
- Avoid repeated summaries, generic study advice, motivational filler, and claims such as “this will definitely be on the exam.”
